Ramayana

Overview

The Ramayana, attributed to the sage Valmiki, is a revered Sanskrit epic of ancient Indian literature. It tells the story of Rama, the prince of Ayodhya, and his quest to rescue his wife Sita from the demon king Ravana. The epic is composed of 24,000 verses divided into seven books (Kandas) and about 500 sargas (cantos).

Key Characters

  • Rama: The protagonist, seventh avatar of the god Vishnu, and prince of Ayodhya.
  • Sita: Rama's devoted wife and the epitome of virtue and chastity.
  • Lakshmana: Rama's loyal brother who accompanies him in exile.
  • Hanuman: The devoted monkey god and ardent devotee of Rama.
  • Ravana: The ten-headed demon king of Lanka who abducts Sita.
  • Dasharatha: The king of Ayodhya and father of Rama.
  • Kaikeyi: One of Dasharatha's queens, whose intrigue leads to Rama's exile.
  • Bharata: Rama’s half-brother who rules Ayodhya during Rama’s exile, vowing to return the kingdom to him.

Story summary

Bala Kanda (Book of Youth)

The Ramayana begins with the birth of Rama and his three brothers to King Dasharatha of Ayodhya. Rama is the eldest and is known for his valor and virtues. The sage Vishwamitra takes Rama and Lakshmana to the forest to protect his yajna (sacrificial ritual) from demons. During this time, Rama wins the hand of Sita, the daughter of King Janaka, by lifting and breaking the bow of Shiva at her swayamvara (a ceremony of choosing a husband).

Ayodhya Kanda (Book of Ayodhya)

King Dasharatha decides to crown Rama as the prince regent, but his plans are thwarted by his youngest queen, Kaikeyi. Influenced by her maid Manthara, Kaikeyi demands that her son Bharata be crowned king and that Rama be exiled to the forest for fourteen years. Bound by his promise to Kaikeyi, Dasharatha reluctantly agrees. Rama, along with Sita and Lakshmana, willingly goes into exile.

Aranya Kanda (Book of the Forest)

During their exile, Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana live in the forests, meeting sages and encountering various demons. Ravana, the demon king of Lanka, hears about Sita’s beauty and devises a plan to abduct her. He sends the demon Maricha, who disguises himself as a golden deer to lure Rama and Lakshmana away from their hermitage. Ravana then kidnaps Sita and takes her to his kingdom in Lanka.

Kishkindha Kanda (Book of Kishkindha)

Rama and Lakshmana search for Sita and meet Hanuman and Sugriva, the monkey king of Kishkindha. Sugriva promises to help Rama in exchange for his assistance in reclaiming his kingdom from his brother Vali. Rama kills Vali, and Sugriva becomes the king. The monkeys, led by Hanuman, begin their search for Sita.

Sundara Kanda (Book of Beauty)

Hanuman leaps across the ocean to Lanka, where he finds Sita in the Ashoka Vatika, guarded by Ravana’s demonesses. He gives her Rama’s ring as a token and assures her that Rama will come to rescue her. Hanuman causes havoc in Lanka, burning parts of the city, before returning to Rama with news of Sita’s location.

Yuddha Kanda (Book of War)

Rama, along with his allies, builds a bridge (Rama Setu) across the ocean to Lanka. A fierce battle ensues between Rama’s army and Ravana’s forces. Rama eventually kills Ravana and rescues Sita. To prove her chastity, Sita undergoes an ordeal by fire (Agni Pariksha) and emerges unscathed, proving her purity.

Uttara Kanda (Book of the Aftermath)

Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana return to Ayodhya, where Rama is crowned king. Despite Sita’s ordeal, rumors about her chastity continue to spread. To uphold his duty as a king, Rama reluctantly banishes the pregnant Sita to the forest. She finds refuge in the ashram of the sage Valmiki, where she gives birth to twin sons, Lava and Kusha. Many years later, Rama and Sita are reunited when Lava and Kusha sing the Ramayana in his court. Sita, however, chooses to return to the earth, her mother, proving her purity once again.

Themes and Significance

  • Dharma: The concept of duty, righteousness, and moral order is central to the Ramayana.
  • Devotion and Loyalty: Demonstrated by characters like Sita, Hanuman, and Lakshmana.
  • Good vs. Evil: The battle between Rama and Ravana symbolizes the eternal struggle between good and evil.
  • The Ideal King: Rama is portrayed as the paragon of an ideal ruler.

Cultural Impact

The Ramayana has had a profound influence on Indian culture, art, and literature. Its themes and characters are deeply ingrained in the social and religious fabric of India. The epic has been retold in various languages and forms, including dance, drama, and television series.

Mahabharata

Overview

The Mahabharata, attributed to the sage Vyasa, is one of the longest epic poems in the world, consisting of over 100,000 shlokas (couplets) divided into 18 parvas (books). It narrates the story of the Kurukshetra War between the Kauravas and the Pandavas, two branches of the Kuru dynasty, and explores themes of duty, righteousness, and the complexities of human nature.

Key Characters

  • Krishna: The incarnation of the god Vishnu, who serves as the charioteer and advisor to Arjuna.
  • Arjuna: The third Pandava brother, a master archer, and the central figure of the Bhagavad Gita.
  • Yudhishthira: The eldest Pandava, known for his adherence to dharma and righteousness.
  • Bhima: The second Pandava, known for his immense strength.
  • Nakula and Sahadeva: The twin Pandava brothers, skilled in various arts and sciences.
  • Draupadi: The shared wife of the Pandavas, known for her beauty, intelligence, and strong will.
  • Duryodhana: The eldest Kaurava and the main antagonist, driven by envy and ambition.
  • Karna: A great warrior, friend of Duryodhana, and rival to Arjuna, with a tragic backstory.
  • Bheeshma: The granduncle of both the Pandavas and Kauravas, known for his vow of celibacy and wisdom.
  • Drona: The royal teacher of the Kauravas and Pandavas, a master of advanced military arts.

Story summary

Adi Parva (Book of the Beginning)

The Mahabharata begins with the history and genealogy of the Kuru dynasty. It narrates the birth and early lives of the Pandavas and Kauravas, their education under the tutelage of Drona, and the rivalry that develops between them. The dice game, orchestrated by Shakuni, leads to the Pandavas' exile and sets the stage for the Kurukshetra War.

Sabha Parva (Book of the Assembly Hall)

The Sabha Parva describes the events leading up to the infamous dice game, where Yudhishthira loses his kingdom, wealth, and even his brothers and wife. The Pandavas are forced into exile for 13 years, with the last year to be spent in incognito.

Vana Parva (Book of the Forest)

During their exile, the Pandavas endure hardships and encounter sages and celestial beings. They gain divine weapons and receive spiritual and moral guidance.

Virata Parva (Book of Virata)

The Pandavas spend their final year of exile in disguise in the kingdom of Virata. They reveal themselves after the year passes, leading to preparations for the great war.

Udyoga Parva (Book of Effort)

Both sides prepare for war, with various attempts at peace negotiations failing. Krishna's role as the diplomat and his famous Bhagavad Gita discourse to Arjuna are central to this book.

Bhishma Parva (Book of Bhishma)

The first ten days of the war are narrated, focusing on Bhishma's leadership of the Kaurava army and his eventual fall on the battlefield.

Drona Parva (Book of Drona)

Drona takes command of the Kaurava forces after Bhishma's fall. Key events include the deaths of Abhimanyu, Jayadratha, and the brutal tactics employed by both sides.

Karṇa Parva (Book of Karṇa)

Karṇa becomes the commander of the Kaurava army. His duel with Arjuna and eventual death are highlights of this book.

Shalya Parva (Book of Shalya)

Shalya leads the Kaurava forces after Karṇa's death. The book details the final days of the war, including the deaths of Duryodhana and other key warriors.

Sauptika Parva (Book of the Sleeping Warriors)

Ashwatthama, Kripa, and Kritavarma launch a night attack on the Pandava camp, leading to the massacre of their sleeping warriors.

Stri Parva (Book of the Women)

The aftermath of the war, focusing on the grief and suffering of the women left behind.

Shanti Parva (Book of Peace)

Yudhishthira's coronation and the moral and philosophical teachings imparted by Bhishma from his deathbed.

Anushasana Parva (Book of Instructions)

Further instructions and guidance from Bhishma on various aspects of life, dharma, and governance.

Ashvamedhika Parva (Book of the Horse Sacrifice)

Yudhishthira performs the Ashvamedha sacrifice to assert his sovereignty and bring peace to his kingdom.

Ashramavasika Parva (Book of the Hermitage)

Dhritarashtra, Gandhari, Kunti, and Vidura retire to the forest, seeking penance and solace.

Mausala Parva (Book of the Clubs)

The end of the Yadava dynasty and the death of Krishna, signaling the end of an era.

Mahaprasthanika Parva (Book of the Great Journey)

The Pandavas renounce their kingdom and embark on their final journey towards the Himalayas.

Svargarohanika Parva (Book of the Ascension to Heaven)

The ascent of the Pandavas to the heavens and Yudhishthira’s final test of virtue and righteousness.

Themes and Significance

  • Dharma: The struggle to uphold righteousness in the face of complex moral dilemmas.
  • Duty and Responsibility: The duties of a warrior, king, and individual towards society and family.
  • Karma: The law of cause and effect, and the consequences of one's actions.
  • Complexity of Human Nature: The multi-dimensional characters and their motivations, virtues, and flaws.

Cultural Impact

The Mahabharata has had a profound impact on Indian culture, philosophy, and literature. Its narratives and teachings have been explored in various art forms, including dance, theatre, and modern media. The Bhagavad Gita, a part of the Mahabharata, is a crucial spiritual text in Hinduism, offering guidance on ethics, duty, and the path to liberation.
